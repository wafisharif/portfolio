NYU ARISE – Lithium-Ion Battery Binder Research

At NYU’s Transformative Materials & Devices Lab, I conducted a comparative study on PVDF vs. CMC binders in graphite anodes for lithium-ion batteries—focusing on sustainability, interfacial stability, and capacity retention.

Abstract:

Polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF), the commercially used lithium-ion battery (LIB) anode binder, relies on the toxic, non-renewable solvent NMP. By contrast, carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) is a water-soluble alternative that improves safety and sustainability. In this study, electrodes were fabricated with 95% graphite and 5% binder, assembled into CR2032 coin cells, and tested using galvanostatic cycling and EIS.

CMC-based anodes exhibited higher reversible capacities (peak 314 mAh/g) compared to PVDF (peak 280 mAh/g), with both achieving high Coulombic efficiencies above 99.7%. At higher C-rates, both cells displayed capacity fade correlated with increased charge-transfer resistance. Preliminary synthesis of lithium-substituted CMC (Li-CMC) suggests potential for improved SEI stability, though full validation is future work.

These findings indicate that while CMC already surpasses PVDF in performance and environmental impact, binder chemistry can be further optimized to improve cycling stability in next-generation batteries.

NYU Machines in Motion Lab – Bio-Inspired Robotics

In the Machines in Motion Lab at NYU Tandon, I worked on bio-inspired locomotion research, focusing on robot designs modeled after penguin biomechanics to optimize movement on low-friction terrain.

  • Developed 3D biomechanical models in OpenSim to simulate penguin-inspired locomotion
  • Designed structural components in SolidWorks
  • Conducted motion-capture kinematics experiments on physical robots to validate simulation models
